# Article 12: Truth and Reconciliation

## Summary

Reconciliation between Canada and Indigenous peoples — First Nations, Métis,
and Inuit — is a legal obligation, a moral commitment, and an unfinished
project. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission's 94 Calls to Action were
published in 2015. The National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous
Women and Girls issued 231 Calls for Justice in 2019. Canada has not completed
the majority of either set of commitments.

This is not a matter of contested priorities. The harms documented by both
inquiries — residential schools, forced family separation, systemic
discrimination, murder and disappearance without adequate investigation —
are matters of public record and federal legal accountability. Reconciliation
requires specific, funded, timely action on specific commitments, not
periodic reaffirmations of goodwill.

This article defines how the PPPC would approach reconciliation: through
the lens of rights, accountability, and co-development. It draws on UNDRIP
(the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples) as
adopted in Canadian law through Bill C-15 in 2021, and on the principle
that Indigenous peoples are rights-holders — not recipients of federal
benevolence.

---

## Principles

**1. UNDRIP implementation is a legal obligation, not a political choice.**
Bill C-15 requires the federal government to align its laws with UNDRIP
and to develop an action plan in consultation with Indigenous peoples.
The action plan and its implementation must be publicly tracked, reported
on, and completed — not deferred indefinitely.

**2. Free, prior, and informed consent is not consultation.**
UNDRIP Article 19 requires that governments obtain the free, prior, and
informed consent of Indigenous peoples before adopting measures that affect
their rights. Consultation that occurs after decisions have been made, or
that treats Indigenous input as advisory rather than rights-based, does not
meet this standard.

**3. Indigenous data sovereignty is a right, not a preference.**
First Nations, Métis nations, and Inuit have rights over data about their
communities and territories. The First Nations Principles of OCAP
(Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) represent the established standard.
Federal programs that collect data about Indigenous communities must
operate with community consent and must transfer data governance to
communities over time.

**4. Co-development means shared authority, not shared meetings.**
Policy that affects Indigenous peoples must be designed with them — not
handed to them for comment after the key decisions are made. Co-development
requires dedicated resources, timelines that accommodate Indigenous
governance processes, and decision points where Indigenous governments
can genuinely shape outcomes.

**5. Material outcomes matter more than process metrics.**
The number of consultations held or meetings attended does not measure
reconciliation. Life expectancy, housing conditions, school completion
rates, access to clean water, safety of Indigenous women and girls — these
are the outcomes that matter. Policy must be evaluated against them.

## Policy Mechanisms

**TRC and MMIWG commitments tracking**
Publish and maintain a publicly accessible register of all TRC Calls to
Action and MMIWG Calls for Justice, with:
- Status: not started / in progress / completed
- Responsible federal departments
- Budget allocated and spent
- Timeline commitment and current projected date
- Links to published output where applicable

The register is updated quarterly. An independent Indigenous-led body
reviews the register annually and publishes its own assessment of progress,
with authority to disagree with government status designations.

**UNDRIP action plan implementation**
The federal UNDRIP action plan is implemented on schedule with:
- Annual public reporting on each action plan commitment
- Independent review by the National Indigenous Rights Advisor or
  an equivalent independent body
- Legislative review schedule for identifying and amending statutes
  inconsistent with UNDRIP, published with timelines

**Safe drinking water — completion, not recommitment**
The federal government has committed multiple times to ending all
drinking water advisories in First Nations communities. Complete this
commitment: fund, build, and operate water and wastewater infrastructure
in all communities on advisory, with community-controlled operations
and long-term federal maintenance commitments. Publish a live status
dashboard for every water advisory, updated in real time.

**MMIWG — National Action Plan implementation**
Implement the Federal Pathway under the National Action Plan to End
Violence Against Indigenous Women, Girls, and 2SLGBTQQIA+ People, with:
- Dedicated funding for family-centred investigative support services
- RCMP case review for unsolved cases meeting published criteria
- Expanded culturally safe crisis services in urban and rural communities
- Annual progress reporting against each pathway commitment

**Indigenous-led service delivery and procurement**
Set and track a target for increasing the share of federal contracts
awarded to Indigenous-owned businesses, using the Procurement Strategy
for Aboriginal Business and successor mechanisms. Support Indigenous
communities in establishing or expanding their own service delivery
capacity for health, education, and social services, with funding that
follows community authority rather than federal program rules.

**Indigenous data sovereignty implementation**
Federal programs collecting data about Indigenous communities must:
- Operate under data sharing agreements developed with community consent
- Include provisions for data transfer to community control over time
- Fund community capacity to manage and use data independently
- Publish which programs have data sovereignty agreements in place and
  the status of those agreements

---

## Measurable Outcomes

- TRC and MMIWG commitment register is live and publicly accessible
  within one year of this article's adoption.
- Zero long-term drinking water advisories in First Nations communities
  within three years of commitment to this article.
- UNDRIP action plan annual progress reports published on schedule
  every year.
- Indigenous-led service delivery and procurement share increases cycle
  over cycle, with data published quarterly.
- MMIWG National Action Plan Federal Pathway annual progress reports
  published on schedule.
- Indigenous data sovereignty agreements in place for 100% of new
  federal programs collecting community-level Indigenous data within
  two years.
- Independent assessment of TRC and MMIWG commitment status published
  annually, with methodology disclosed.
