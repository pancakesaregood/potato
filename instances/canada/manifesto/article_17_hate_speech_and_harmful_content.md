# Article 17 — Hate Speech and Harmful Content

## Summary

Canada's existing legal framework prohibits the most severe forms of hate
speech: public incitement to genocide (Criminal Code s. 318), public
advocacy of hatred against identifiable groups (s. 319(1)), and wilful
promotion of hatred (s. 319(2)). These provisions represent the minimum
floor. They do not address the full range of harmful content that causes
demonstrable injury to individuals and communities — content that falls
below the criminal threshold but still erodes participation in public life,
targets people on the basis of protected characteristics, and amplifies
discrimination.

At the same time, the Charter guarantee of freedom of expression
(s. 2(b)) is not a technicality. It reflects a genuine public interest in
open debate, including debate on contested and uncomfortable questions. A
framework that conflates criticism, satire, and offensive speech with
incitement causes its own harms: chilling legitimate dissent, enabling
selective enforcement, and concentrating moderation authority in ways that
are themselves prone to abuse.

This article describes a policy framework that takes both concerns
seriously. The goal is not to resolve the tension by eliminating one side
of it — it is to manage the tension through proportionate, transparent,
and independently accountable mechanisms.

---

## Principles

**1. Harmful speech and free expression are both genuine values.**
Neither can be treated as a pretextual concern. Policy that addresses one
by eliminating the other is not a balance — it is a choice with
consequences that must be owned explicitly.

**2. Harm must be demonstrable and specific.**
Vague or subjective definitions of harmful content create enforcement
discretion that is structurally prone to abuse. Harm thresholds must be
grounded in evidence of actual injury — psychological, social, economic —
to real people and communities, not in the offensiveness of ideas to
majorities or institutions.

**3. Enforcement mechanisms must be independent and contestable.**
Who decides what constitutes harmful content matters as much as the
definition. Enforcement by platforms, governments, or dominant groups
without independent oversight reproduces existing power imbalances.
Decisions must be transparent, reasons must be published, and appeals
must be available.

**4. Targeted communities are primary stakeholders.**
People who are the subjects of hate speech — not regulators, platforms,
or researchers — are the primary experts on its effects. Policy design
must center their experience and include their meaningful participation
in governance of enforcement systems.

**5. Prevention is more effective than removal.**
Content moderation is reactive. Education, counter-speech programs,
media literacy, and structural supports for targeted communities reduce
harm more durably than removal-after-publication. Both have a role; the
policy emphasis should be on prevention.

## Policy Mechanisms

### 1. Clarify and codify the harm threshold

Amend the Criminal Code and human rights legislation to provide a clearer,
evidence-based definition of harmful content that distinguishes:

- **Criminal hate speech**: content that advocates genocide, incites violence
  against identifiable groups, or wilfully promotes hatred with the purpose
  or foreseeable effect of encouraging discrimination or violence. Existing
  Criminal Code provisions (ss. 318–319) are the baseline; enforcement gaps
  should be addressed through prosecutorial resources and training, not by
  expanding definitions without corresponding procedural safeguards.

- **Civil hate speech**: content that targets individuals or groups on the
  basis of protected characteristics in ways that cause demonstrable harm
  short of the criminal threshold. This category is addressed through the
  Canadian Human Rights Act and provincial equivalents, with remedies
  including removal, correction, and compensation — not criminal sanction.

- **Offensive but protected speech**: content that is offensive, distressing,
  or morally objectionable but does not meet the harm threshold for either
  category above. This content is protected under Charter s. 2(b) and is
  not subject to state removal, regardless of how widely it is condemned.

Definitions must be published, regularly reviewed against evidence of
harm and enforcement patterns, and subject to parliamentary oversight.

### 2. Platform accountability with procedural requirements

Large digital platforms operating in Canada that host user-generated
content must:

- Publish clear, plain-language community standards that describe what
  content is prohibited, with specific examples, and how prohibited content
  is defined relative to the legal categories above.
- Provide a transparent, timely appeals process for content removal
  decisions, with reasons given in writing for each removal.
- Publish quarterly transparency reports covering: volume of removal
  requests by category, removal rates, appeal outcomes, and demographic
  data on affected users where available.
- Designate a Canadian point of contact responsible for compliance with
  Canadian law and for responding to enforcement requests.

Platforms are not required to remove content below the civil hate speech
threshold. Voluntary removal decisions must still be governed by published
standards and be subject to an appeals process.

### 3. Independent oversight body

Establish an independent Digital Content Oversight Commissioner with:

- Mandate to investigate complaints about platform enforcement failures
  (including both under-enforcement and over-enforcement).
- Authority to audit platform compliance with transparency requirements.
- Power to issue binding orders for procedural compliance (transparency
  reporting, appeals provision), not binding orders to remove specific content.
- Membership that includes representatives of communities targeted by hate
  speech, civil liberties organizations, and technical experts — not
  predominantly government appointees or platform representatives.
- Annual public reporting to Parliament.

The Commissioner does not have the power to order content removed. That
power remains with courts and the Canadian Human Rights Tribunal.

### 4. Counter-speech and media literacy investment

Fund a sustained national program of:

- Counter-speech grants to civil society organizations, with priority
  for organizations led by communities targeted by hate speech.
- Media literacy curriculum integrated into K–12 education, covering
  the mechanics of disinformation, the difference between offensive and
  harmful speech, and how algorithmic amplification works.
- Research funding for independent study of the effects of harmful content
  on targeted communities, the effectiveness of different intervention
  models, and the patterns of over- and under-enforcement.

### 5. Safeguards against enforcement abuse

Every enforcement mechanism must include explicit protections against
pretextual use:

- Enforcement decisions must be documented with specific reasons tied to
  the published harm definitions — not to the general undesirability of
  the content.
- Patterns of enforcement that disproportionately affect particular
  political viewpoints, racial groups, or protected classes are grounds
  for audit by the Oversight Commissioner.
- Any expansion of hate speech definitions or enforcement powers must be
  preceded by a public consultation of at least 90 days that specifically
  solicits input from civil liberties organizations and targeted communities.

---

## Measurable Outcomes

- Harmful content definitions are published, publicly available, and
  reviewed on a three-year cycle against enforcement outcome data.
- All major platforms operating in Canada publish quarterly transparency
  reports within 18 months of legislation passing.
- The Digital Content Oversight Commissioner is operational within two
  years of legislation, with a public-facing complaints portal.
- Counter-speech and media literacy programs reach at least 500,000
  Canadians annually within three years of program launch.
- Appeal processes for platform content decisions are available and
  resolve within 30 days for 90% of cases.
- Annual independent measurement of hate speech prevalence on major
  platforms, with trend data published publicly.
- Enforcement pattern audits conducted annually; results published with
  any corrective actions taken.
