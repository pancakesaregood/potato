# Article 13: Symbolism of Brown

## Summary

The Peoples Potato Party of Canada chose the colour brown deliberately.
It is the colour of soil — the material on which food is grown, the
substrate of ecosystems, the thing that connects agriculture to ecology
to labour to sustenance. It is not the most glamorous political colour.
That is part of the point.

Canadian politics has an aesthetic tendency toward either the triumphalist
(flags, heroes, national mythology) or the abstract (values, frameworks,
visions). The PPPC identifies with neither. Brown is the colour of the
actual ground under actual feet — unglamorous, essential, and shared.

This article explains the symbol, clarifies what it does not mean, and
connects it to the policy work it is meant to represent.

---

## Principles

**1. The symbol is about soil, food, and labour.**
Brown in the PPPC context means: the earth that food grows in, the work
of growing and feeding, the unglamorous practical maintenance of things
that matter. It is a deliberate departure from political symbols that
invoke power, purity, or glory.

**2. The symbol must be clearly interpreted to prevent confusion.**
Brown has been used in other political contexts with racial or ethnic
connotations that the PPPC explicitly rejects. The party does not use
"brown" to refer to any racial or ethnic group, and does not accept
political uses of the symbol that imply racial hierarchy, exclusion,
or pride in characteristics rather than commitments. The interpretive
note below is part of the party's public identity, not a footnote.

**3. Symbolism should be earned by policy, not asserted by rhetoric.**
A party that claims to represent working people, food systems, and
ecological stewardship should be evaluated by whether its policies
actually serve those things. The symbol is a commitment, not a costume.
Articles 05, 06, 10, and 12 are where the symbol's meaning is tested.

**4. Symbols should be accessible across cultures and languages.**
The PPPC operates in a multilingual, multicultural country with
diverse relationships to land, soil, and food. The symbol of brown must
be interpreted in ways that are resonant and respectful across those
cultures — not through a single settler-agricultural lens that excludes
other relationships to the land.

---

## Interpretive Note

The PPPC's use of brown as a party colour refers exclusively to soil, earth,
food systems, and physical labour. It carries no reference to, and does not
endorse, any racial classification, ethnic identity, or hierarchy based on
skin colour or ancestry.

The party acknowledges that brown has been used in other political contexts
to signal racial exclusivity. The PPPC explicitly rejects this use. Any
application of the party's brown symbolism in ways that evoke racial
hierarchy — including in party materials, merchandise, or public communications —
is inconsistent with the party's values and subject to correction under the
Code of Conduct.

---

## Policy Mechanisms

**Consistent visual identity with published interpretation**
The PPPC maintains a visual identity guide that defines the use of brown
in party materials, explains its meaning in plain language, includes the
interpretive note above as a required element, and distinguishes the
symbol from racial or ethnic uses with which it might be confused.
The guide is publicly available and updated at each governance cycle.

**Symbol-linked policy campaigns**
The party connects the brown symbol to substantive policy work through:
- Soil health and agricultural sustainability campaigns (Article 06,
  Article 10)
- Food security and food system resilience (Article 16)
- Labour dignity and worker rights (Articles 03, 07, 08)
- Indigenous land stewardship relationships (Article 12)

Campaigns that use the brown symbol without connection to substantive
policy content are subject to review by the party's communications
governance process.

**Multilingual and multicultural symbol interpretation**
The party's interpretation of the brown symbol is translated into
Canada's official languages and, where resources allow, into languages
relevant to specific communities the party is engaging with. Cultural
advisors from Indigenous communities and other communities with specific
relationships to land and soil are consulted on whether the symbol's
presentation is respectful and resonant in their context.

---

## Measurable Outcomes

- Visual identity guide with interpretive note is published and publicly
  available within one governance cycle of this article's adoption.
- Zero approved party communications use the brown symbol in ways
  that reference racial or ethnic identity.
- Symbol-linked policy campaigns are connected to a published policy
  article in every governance cycle.
- Multilingual interpretive materials are available in both official
  languages within two years.
- Community feedback on symbol interpretation from Indigenous advisory
  processes is documented and used in periodic guide updates.
