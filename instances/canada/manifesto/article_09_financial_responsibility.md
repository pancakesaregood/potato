# Article 09: Financial Responsibility

## Summary

Fiscal policy shapes what government can do and what it cannot. A government
that borrows recklessly — committing future revenues to service debt rather
than public services — limits the choices of future Canadians. A government
that refuses to invest — treating all spending as suspect, cutting programs
that build long-term productivity — also limits them, differently.

The PPPC holds neither austerity nor unlimited spending as a principle. It
holds fiscal transparency, honest costing, outcome-based program evaluation,
and clear rules for when and how much borrowing is appropriate. The goal is
a fiscal policy that citizens can evaluate and hold to account — not one
that obscures tradeoffs behind opaque assumptions or selective framing.

Canada's federal fiscal situation is manageable by developed-country
standards. The risks are specific: large and growing tax expenditures that
are rarely evaluated, program spending that accumulates without systematic
review, and fiscal projections that routinely use assumptions that are not
stress-tested publicly. These are tractable problems with known solutions.

---

## Principles

**1. Budgets are public documents that must be readable.**
A budget that only specialists can interpret is not a democratic document —
it is a technical artefact that forecloses public accountability. Plain-language
budget summaries, standardised tables, and accessible online presentation
are minimum requirements, not optional improvements.

**2. Tax expenditures are spending and must be evaluated as spending.**
A tax credit or deduction forgoes revenue that would otherwise fund public
services. Tax expenditures total over $200 billion annually in Canada and
are evaluated far less rigorously than direct spending. There is no principled
reason for this asymmetry.

**3. Borrowing rules must be explicit and honest about their purpose.**
Borrowing to finance durable productive investment — infrastructure, education,
research capacity — is different from borrowing to cover operating costs or
provide politically convenient benefits before elections. The distinction
matters and should be reflected in fiscal rules.

**4. Program review must be routine, not crisis-driven.**
Programs accumulate. Some outlive their purpose. Some produce outcomes that
differ from their stated objectives. Systematic review is not an ideological
commitment to cutting — it is a governance obligation to ensure public money
achieves public value.

**5. Fiscal projections must acknowledge uncertainty.**
Economic forecasting is uncertain. Budgets built on single-scenario projections
that do not disclose sensitivity to growth, interest rate, or commodity
assumptions create artificial confidence. Stress-case and base-case scenarios
should both be published.

## Policy Mechanisms

**Plain-language budget requirements**
Federal budgets and fiscal updates are published simultaneously with a
plain-language summary (maximum 20 pages) that: explains the major spending
and revenue decisions, identifies the three largest fiscal risks, states
the key economic assumptions and their uncertainty range, and compares
outcomes against the previous year's projections. The summary is published
alongside the full technical budget documents, not as a replacement.

**Tax expenditure review programme**
Commission a rolling review of federal tax expenditures on a five-year cycle,
with 20% of expenditures reviewed each year. Reviews use the same framework
as major spending program evaluations: stated objective, evidence of
effectiveness, distributional impact, and recommendation to continue, modify,
or eliminate. Reviews are published in full. Parliamentary committee review
of findings is required before tax expenditures above $500 million annually
are continued without modification.

**Multi-year fiscal framework with stress-case scenarios**
Federal fiscal plans cover a rolling five-year horizon. Each plan publishes
three scenarios: a base case, a stress case (using independently validated
downside assumptions), and a fiscal resilience scenario showing the impact
of a major external shock equivalent to the most severe recession in the
prior 30 years. Assumptions used in each scenario are disclosed.

**Borrowing framework**
Federal borrowing is governed by a published framework distinguishing:
capital borrowing (for assets with useful lives greater than 10 years,
permissible within defined debt-to-GDP parameters), operational deficit
financing (permissible in recession and crisis conditions, with automatic
consolidation pathway), and emergency borrowing (requiring parliamentary
declaration and sunset). The framework is reviewed and updated at each
national convention cycle.

**Program evaluation and sunset provisions**
New spending programs above $100 million annually include, at creation:
a stated objective, a five-year evaluation date, measurable outcomes, and
a sunset clause unless renewed through evaluation. Existing programs
without evaluation dates are assigned to a catch-up review schedule
published within one year. Evaluation results are published in full.
Programs that cannot demonstrate progress against their objectives within
two evaluation cycles are referred to Treasury Board for termination or
redesign.

**Independent fiscal analysis**
The Parliamentary Budget Officer's mandate is confirmed and resourced
to provide independent analysis of all major spending proposals, tax
expenditure valuations, and economic projections used in budget documents.
PBO analysis is released publicly before parliamentary votes on budget
implementation. The PBO's operating budget is set by parliamentary
resolution, not Treasury Board appropriation.

---

## Measurable Outcomes

- Plain-language budget summary published with every federal budget and
  fiscal update, beginning in the first budget year of this article's adoption.
- Tax expenditure review programme is operational within two years, with
  annual review outputs published.
- Multi-year fiscal plans with stress-case scenarios published in every
  budget cycle.
- Program evaluation backlog (existing programs without evaluation dates)
  is published and assigned to a schedule within one year.
- 100% of new spending programs above $100 million created after adoption
  include sunset clauses and five-year evaluation dates.
- Parliamentary Budget Officer analysis published before votes on all
  major budget implementation legislation.
