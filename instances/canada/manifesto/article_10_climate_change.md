# Article 10: Climate Change

## Summary

Climate change is the defining long-range policy challenge of this generation.
The physical changes — rising temperatures, shifting precipitation, more
frequent and severe extreme weather events, sea level rise — are no longer
projections. They are current conditions, accelerating. The policy question
is not whether to respond but how to allocate scarce public resources between
mitigation (reducing emissions to limit further change) and adaptation
(preparing for the change that is already locked in).

Canada is both a significant emitter and a country with exceptional exposure
to climate impacts: wildfire, permafrost thaw, coastal erosion, freshwater
stress, and extreme heat events in communities that are not built for them.
This article covers both sides — the emissions pathway and the adaptation
obligation — and insists that both must be funded, governed, and reported
on honestly.

Article 06 covers broader environmental stewardship. This article is
specifically about climate policy: emissions, carbon pricing, clean
energy transition, and adaptation planning.

---

## Principles

**1. Emissions targets must be credible and legally binding.**
Voluntary targets without enforcement mechanisms do not work. Canada's record
on climate commitments includes consistent failure to meet announced targets.
The credibility problem is structural: targets that are easy to abandon do
not drive the investment and regulatory changes required to meet them.
Legally binding targets with defined compliance mechanisms are different
in kind, not just degree.

**2. Carbon pricing is effective and should be maintained.**
The evidence on carbon pricing is clear: it reduces emissions, it drives
innovation, and it can be designed to be revenue-neutral or progressive.
Political pressure against carbon pricing should not determine science and
economics policy. The mechanism should be defended, improved, and made
more equitable — not abandoned.

**3. Transition costs must be distributed fairly.**
Moving away from fossil fuel production and consumption creates real economic
disruption for workers and communities that have built their livelihoods
around those industries. Transition support is not a political concession —
it is a precondition for durable climate policy. Workers in affected
industries deserve retraining, income support, and investment in their
communities.

**4. Adaptation is an obligation, not an afterthought.**
Communities already experiencing climate impacts — from Northern First Nations
living with permafrost disruption, to coastal communities facing storm surge,
to prairie farmers facing drought — cannot wait for mitigation to work.
Adaptation investment must be targeted, evidence-based, and begin immediately.

**5. Emissions data must be transparent and independently verified.**
Canada's National Inventory Report covers most major emission sources but
relies on self-reported data from industries with obvious incentives to
minimise figures. Independent verification, remote sensing data integration,
and transparent methodology are required for the inventory to be credible
as a policy tool.

## Policy Mechanisms

**Legally binding emissions pathway**
Amend the Canadian Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act to add:
binding interim milestones with consequences for non-compliance (including
required remediation plans reviewed by Parliament), mandatory independent
review of sectoral emissions plans, and independent expert review of
milestone compliance before new target-setting. Emissions plans for each
major sector (electricity, oil and gas, transportation, buildings,
agriculture) are published and updated annually.

**Carbon pricing: maintenance and equity improvement**
Maintain the federal carbon pricing system and resist rollback in response
to political pressure. Improve equity by ensuring the rebate mechanism
is calibrated to offset costs for lower-income households, publishing
distributional impact analyses annually, and reviewing fuel charge exemptions
for agricultural and industrial uses against emissions reduction evidence.

**Clean electricity standard**
Implement a Clean Electricity Standard requiring the federal electricity
grid (and provincial grids receiving federal investment) to reach net-zero
or near-zero emissions by 2035. Federal investment in transmission
infrastructure, storage, and clean generation is conditional on adherence
to this standard. Jurisdictions that do not meet the standard are ineligible
for federal clean power investment beyond the transition period.

**Building retrofit programme**
Scale federal support for building retrofits to reach deep emissions
reductions in the residential and commercial building stock, which
accounts for 13% of national emissions. The programme combines:
upfront financing (loans repayable through utility savings), grants for
low-income households, training pipelines for retrofit tradespeople, and
streamlined permitting for heat pump and insulation upgrades. Annual
targets for retrofits completed, disaggregated by building type and income
level.

**Just transition framework**
Establish a permanent Just Transition Fund with long-term commitments
to support workers and communities in fossil fuel-dependent industries
through: transition income support for displaced workers, retraining
programs co-designed with unions and affected communities, community
economic diversification investment, and pension security for workers
with legacy obligations in shutting facilities. Fund governance includes
elected worker representatives and community members from affected regions.

**Adaptation planning — federal obligation**
Require all federal departments to publish adaptation plans for the
climate risks most relevant to their operations and mandates. The National
Adaptation Strategy is updated on a three-year cycle with mandatory
community-level climate risk assessments for high-risk communities (those
facing wildfire, coastal flood, extreme heat, or permafrost risk) within
two years of this article's adoption. Federal adaptation transfers to
provinces are conditioned on published adaptation plans meeting minimum
standards.

**Emissions inventory — independent verification**
Commission a three-year programme to integrate satellite and remote-sensing
data into the National Inventory Report for major emission sources (oil and
gas, agriculture, landfills). Publish a comparison of reported versus
independently estimated emissions with methodology disclosed. Where
significant discrepancies are identified, require sector-level audits.

## Measurable Outcomes

- National emissions trend downward against each legally binding interim
  milestone, with published compliance reports.
- Carbon pricing system maintained through the commitment period, with
  annual equity impact analysis published.
- Clean electricity standard compliance rate by jurisdiction published
  annually, with investment eligibility determinations transparent.
- Building retrofit programme reaches measurable annual targets by
  building type, with income-disaggregated data published.
- Just Transition Fund expenditure and outcomes published annually:
  workers supported, retraining completions, community investment projects.
- Federal departmental adaptation plans published within 18 months,
  high-risk community risk assessments complete within two years.
- Independent emissions verification programme operational within
  three years, comparison data published.
