# Article 11: Freelander Citizenship

## Summary

The Freelander concept explores whether a form of portable civic affiliation —
distinct from and subordinate to legal citizenship — could expand democratic
participation for people who are not currently full participants in Canadian
governance: permanent residents not yet eligible for citizenship, diaspora
members, and people engaged with Canadian civic life across digital channels.

This is an exploratory article. The Freelander concept is not implemented
policy — it is a design space that the PPPC is examining because it is
consistent with the party's founding commitment to expanding democratic
participation beyond its current limits.

The legal, technical, and equity risks are substantial and must be addressed
before any operational model is considered. This article does not authorise
implementation. It defines the principles and process for rigorous evaluation.

---

## Principles

**1. Participation must expand, not stratify.**
Any new civic status must complement existing rights, not create a
two-tier system in which some people's participation is treated as less
legitimate. A model that positions Freelander status as a stepping stone
to real citizenship risks doing exactly that. Design must prevent this.

**2. Legal clarity precedes any operational model.**
Canada's Constitution, the Canada Elections Act, and the Citizenship Act
define who may participate in what forms of civic life. A Freelander model
that touches any of these boundaries requires a complete legal analysis
before any pilot is designed. Political enthusiasm is not a substitute for
constitutional compliance.

**3. Privacy and consent are non-negotiable from the start.**
Civic participation involves political opinions, which are special-category
personal data under data protection principles (see also: Article 03,
Equality and Rights; the core governance kernel, Section 8). A Freelander
identity system that collects, retains, or discloses this data without
genuine informed consent and robust privacy-by-design would undermine the
very participation it purports to support.

**4. The system must work for people with limited digital access.**
A purely digital civic participation model excludes people who lack
reliable internet access, digital literacy, or compatible devices — often
the same communities whose participation is most underserved by current
systems. Accessibility is a design constraint, not a post-launch add-on.

**5. Any model requires explicit parliamentary and public authorisation.**
The PPPC does not have the authority to create new legal statuses through
internal governance documents. Parliamentary legislation, informed by
genuine public consultation, is required before any Freelander model
could be used for anything beyond informal consultation.

## Policy Mechanisms

**Phase 1: Legal and technical scoping review**
Commission an independent legal and technical review of the Freelander
concept covering:
- Constitutional and statutory limits on non-citizen participation in
  federal governance processes
- Data protection requirements for civic identity systems under PIPEDA
  and provincial privacy law
- Technical options for a portable, privacy-respecting civic identity
  that does not duplicate or compete with existing government identity
  infrastructure
- International precedents for expanded civic participation by non-citizens
  or diaspora communities
- Equity analysis of which populations would benefit and which would be
  excluded by various design options

The review is published in full and subject to 90-day public comment.

**Phase 2: Consultation-only pilot**
If the legal review identifies a permissible and bounded scope, a pilot
may proceed limited to: consultation on policy proposals, feedback on
manifesto drafts, and participation in non-binding deliberative processes.
No voting, no decision-making authority. The pilot:
- Operates under a published data protection agreement aligned with
  the governance kernel's Personal Data primitive
- Requires explicit informed consent at enrolment, including clear
  explanation of what data is collected and how it is used
- Runs for a defined period with independent evaluation before any
  extension is considered
- Publishes aggregate participation data and privacy audit results

**Phase 3: Parliamentary process**
Any extension of the Freelander concept beyond consultation — including
any form of deliberative authority, advisory votes, or status recognised
outside the PPPC's own governance — requires federal legislation passed
through Parliament with public committee hearings and rights-impact
assessment under Article 03 procedures.

**Ongoing: participant data rights**
Participants in any Freelander pilot have full rights over their data
consistent with PIPEDA and the governance kernel's Section 8 Data
Governance Agreement, including:
- Right to access all data held about them
- Right to correct inaccurate data
- Right to erasure of personal identifying data on request
  (with governance-act records pseudonymised but retained, consistent
  with the transparency-log provisions in the governance kernel)
- Right to data portability in a machine-readable format

---

## Measurable Outcomes

- Independent legal and technical scoping review commissioned within
  one year of this article's adoption and published with findings.
- Public comment period completed and responses summarised before
  any pilot design decision is made.
- If a consultation pilot proceeds: privacy audit published before
  launch, aggregate participation data published quarterly, independent
  evaluation published at pilot close.
- Zero instances of Freelander status being used to make or record
  binding decisions on matters that require Canadian citizenship
  under existing law.
- Data protection agreement for any pilot is reviewed against the
  governance kernel's Section 8 requirements before launch, with
  review outcome published.
