# Article 11: Freelander Citizenship

## Summary

The Freelander initiative creates a voluntary civic tier for Canadians who
choose to opt out of the tax system in exchange for foregoing access to
publicly-funded services. Freelanders remain full Canadian citizens with
all rights guaranteed by the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms and
the Constitution Act — no exceptions. Their democratic participation,
including the right to vote, is unaffected.

In practical terms, a Freelander pays no federal or provincial income tax,
no GST/HST, and no municipal rates — but in return does not draw on
services funded by those revenues. Public roads become toll roads for
Freelanders. They are not enrolled in public healthcare, public education,
or social assistance programs. They fund their own infrastructure, their
own utilities, and their own community services.

The intent is to create a genuine opt-out path for Canadians who wish to
live more self-sufficiently — particularly those building small rural
communities, bush settlements, or off-grid homesteads — without requiring
them to leave Canada or renounce their citizenship.

Freelander status is reversible. A Freelander may re-enroll in the
standard civic system at any time, resuming tax obligations and regaining
access to public services from that point forward.

---

## Principles

**1. Charter and constitutional rights are absolute and non-negotiable.**
No aspect of Freelander status diminishes any right guaranteed by the
Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms or the Constitution Act. Courts,
legal aid, fundamental justice, equality rights, and democratic rights
remain fully intact. The opt-out is fiscal and service-based only.

**2. Voting rights are fully preserved.**
Freelander status has no effect on electoral participation. Freelanders
vote in federal, provincial, and municipal elections on the same basis
as any other citizen. Civic voice is not contingent on tax contribution.

**3. The opt-out is bilateral and transparent.**
Opting out means genuinely opting out — no hidden subsidies, no back-door
access to services not paid for. Equally, the state makes no claim on
a Freelander's income, assets, or labour beyond what is necessary to
administer their status and protect their constitutional rights.

**4. Re-enrollment must be accessible and unconditional.**
A Freelander who chooses to return to the standard civic system may do so
without penalty, without a waiting period beyond what is operationally
necessary, and without forfeiture of assets accumulated during their
Freelander period. The door back is always open.

**5. The system should encourage — not penalise — rural and community life.**
The Freelander framework is expected to have a natural gravitational pull
toward small rural communities, bush settlements, and co-operative
homesteads, where self-sufficiency is most viable. Policy design should
actively support this outcome, not treat it as an edge case.

---

## Policy Mechanisms

**Opt-out registration**
A Canadian citizen may apply for Freelander status through a federal
registration process. On confirmation:
- Federal and provincial income tax obligations cease from the registration
  date forward
- GST/HST liability on personal transactions ceases
- Enrollment in public healthcare (provincial), CPP, and EI is suspended
- Municipal rates and public utility entitlements are addressed through
  an agreement with the relevant municipality (see below)

**Toll and user-pay access**
Freelanders access publicly-built infrastructure on a user-pay basis:
- Road tolls apply on public highways and maintained municipal roads
- Access to public utilities (water, sewage, waste collection) is available
  at cost-recovery rates with no subsidy component
- Public transit fares reflect full cost recovery
- Where no toll or user-pay mechanism exists for a given service,
  the default is that the service is not available to Freelanders
  until one is established

**Municipal agreements**
Municipalities are empowered to negotiate bilateral agreements with
registered Freelanders or Freelander communities covering local service
access, road maintenance contributions, and emergency access arrangements.
These agreements are voluntary on both sides and publicly registered.

**Charter and constitutional protections — no carve-outs**
The following are explicitly preserved regardless of Freelander status,
with no exceptions permitted by any implementing legislation or regulation:
- All rights and freedoms under the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms
- Emergency services response (police, fire, ambulance) — cost recovery
  may be pursued after the fact where reasonable, but service cannot be
  withheld in an emergency
- Access to the court system and legal process
- Military and national defence
- Any right that cannot lawfully be waived under the Constitution Act

**Re-enrollment**
A Freelander who wishes to return to standard civic status submits a
re-enrollment declaration. Tax obligations resume from the next applicable
filing period. Public service entitlements (healthcare enrollment,
CPP/EI participation) resume according to the rules of each program.
No penalty, surcharge, or asset assessment applies to the transition.

---

## Measurable Outcomes

- Freelander registration process established and operational within
  two years of this article's adoption as PPPC policy.
- Municipal agreement framework published and available before
  registration opens.
- Annual public report on: number of registered Freelanders, geographic
  distribution, re-enrollment rates, and any Charter-related legal
  proceedings arising from the program.
- Zero instances of Charter or constitutional rights being withheld
  from a registered Freelander.
- Independent review of the program at five years, with findings
  published and tabled in Parliament.
