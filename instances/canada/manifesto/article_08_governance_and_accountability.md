# Article 08: Governance and Accountability

## Summary

This article addresses how federal government itself should operate: how
decisions are made and recorded, how contracts are awarded and disclosed,
how public servants are protected when they report wrongdoing, and how
citizens can verify that institutions are doing what they say.

The PPPC's commitment to governance-as-code — version-controlled, transparent,
auditable policy — is a direct expression of these values applied to the
party itself. The same principles apply to government: decisions made through
documented processes, reasons published, records retained, corrections made
visibly. This is not idealism. It is the minimum infrastructure for a
functional democracy.

Accountability has three parts: disclosure (people can see what is happening),
oversight (independent bodies can evaluate it), and consequence (when
obligations are not met, something happens). All three are necessary. Disclosure
without oversight is noise. Oversight without consequence is theatre. The
Canadian federal system has made progress on disclosure but has persistent
weaknesses in independent oversight and meaningful consequence.

---

## Principles

**1. Public office is a trust with documented obligations.**
Ministers, senior officials, and members of Parliament exercise public power
on behalf of citizens. The rules governing that power — conflicts of interest,
lobbying, post-employment restrictions, expense reporting — must be published,
clear, and enforced by bodies with genuine independence.

**2. Procurement must be open to scrutiny.**
Government contracting involves large sums and significant risk of political
influence, favouritism, and fraud. Open contracting — publishing contracts,
amendments, and performance data in machine-readable formats — is the
standard that reduces these risks. Canada has adopted open contracting
principles; implementation remains incomplete.

**3. Whistleblowers are a public accountability mechanism.**
Public servants who report wrongdoing take personal risk in the public interest.
The Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act has been evaluated consistently
as failing to protect them. Reform is not optional — it is overdue.

**4. Algorithmic government is not exempt from accountability.**
Federal agencies increasingly use automated systems to make or inform
decisions about citizens. These systems carry the same obligation of
documented rationale, audit trail, and appeal pathway as human decision-
making. Article 03 (Equality and Rights) establishes the algorithmic
accountability framework; this article applies it to administrative
processes broadly.

**5. Service quality is measurable and should be measured.**
Federal departments deliver services — passport processing, benefit
adjudication, permit approval, tax administration — that Canadians depend
on. Service standards should be set, published, and met. When they are not
met, the gap should be reported and addressed. Citizens should not need
access to information legislation to find out how long things take.

## Policy Mechanisms

**Open contracting implementation**
Mandate publication of all federal contracts above $10,000 in the Open
Contracting Data Standard (OCDS) machine-readable format, including:
contract value, awarded supplier, procurement method, amendments and
change orders, and final delivery or completion status. Contracts are
published within 30 days of award and updated within 30 days of amendment
or completion. Compliance is audited annually by the Auditor General.

**Lobbying transparency expansion**
Require registration and monthly disclosure for all lobbying activity
directed at federal ministers, parliamentary secretaries, senior officials,
and Members of Parliament — including communications that do not currently
trigger registration. Disclosure includes meeting dates, participants,
subject matter, and outcomes where identifiable. The Lobbying Commissioner's
investigation and penalty authority is strengthened to include meaningful
fines and public disclosure of findings.

**Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act reform**
Commission and publish an independent evaluation of the Public Servants
Disclosure Protection Act within 18 months. Implement recommendations
including: restructuring the Public Sector Integrity Commissioner as
a genuinely independent officer of Parliament, eliminating procedural
barriers to complaint investigation, and providing whistleblowers with
legal representation support. Track the number of disclosures received,
investigated, and resulting in findings, published annually.

**Mandate letters and delivery tracking**
Publish ministerial mandate letters on appointment and update them at
each governance cycle. Track and publish progress against mandate
commitments in a standardised public dashboard. Where commitments are
not met, departments publish a reason and revised timeline or a
decision to close the commitment.

**Service standards — publication and tracking**
Require all federal departments delivering citizen-facing services to
publish service standards (processing times, response times, error rates)
and track performance against them in real time or near-real time on
public-facing dashboards. Standards are set at levels that reflect actual
citizen needs, not administrative convenience. Where standards are not
met for two consecutive quarters, departments must publish a remediation
plan reviewed by the Treasury Board Secretariat.

**Independent ethics and conflict oversight**
The Conflict of Interest and Ethics Commissioner and the Lobbying
Commissioner receive operating budgets set by parliamentary resolution
rather than through Treasury Board appropriations, reducing dependence on
government goodwill. Findings are automatically tabled in Parliament
within 30 days of completion. Recommendations are tracked to government
response and implementation status, published publicly.

**Audit finding remediation**
Auditor General findings are tracked in a public register. Departments
must respond to findings within 60 days with a remediation plan. Plans
are followed up in subsequent audit cycles. Where remediation plans are
not implemented within the stated timeline, the non-compliance is included
in the Auditor General's annual public reporting.

## Measurable Outcomes

- 100% of federal contracts above $10,000 are published in OCDS format
  within two years of policy implementation.
- Lobbying disclosure registration rates increase and monthly disclosure
  compliance reaches 95% within two years.
- PSDA reform is implemented within three years, with independent evaluation
  of effectiveness at the five-year mark.
- Service standard dashboards are live for 100% of major federal services
  within two years.
- Auditor General finding remediation rate — percentage of findings
  closed within stated timelines — improves cycle over cycle, published
  in annual reporting.
- Ethics Commissioner and Lobbying Commissioner annual reports tabled
  in Parliament within the legislated timelines in every reporting year.
