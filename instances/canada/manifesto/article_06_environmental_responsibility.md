# Article 06: Environmental Responsibility

## Summary

Environmental responsibility encompasses more than reducing carbon emissions.
It includes the protection and restoration of land, water, and biodiversity;
the accountability of those who damage ecosystems; and the long-term
stewardship obligations that flow from using a shared environment.

Canada has extensive federal jurisdiction over fisheries, navigable waters,
and interprovincial environmental effects. It also controls environmental
conditions on federal lands, in national parks, and through federal
environmental assessment. This article is about how that jurisdiction should
be exercised and what it should be accountable to.

Climate change is addressed in Article 10. This article covers the broader
ecology of environmental stewardship: contaminated sites, water quality,
waste, biodiversity, species at risk, and the polluter-pays principle.

---

## Principles

**1. The polluter-pays principle is not negotiable.**
Those who cause environmental damage bear the cost of remediation. Federal
environmental liability rules must close the gaps that allow responsible
parties to abandon contaminated sites, declare bankruptcy, or negotiate
settlements that shift cleanup costs to the public.

**2. Restoration is as important as protection.**
Preventing new damage and repairing past damage are both obligations. Canada
has a substantial liability of historic contamination — industrial sites,
abandoned mines, municipal waste — that has been systematically under-funded.
Restoration has a price; deferring it makes that price larger.

**3. Biodiversity loss is a crisis that deserves the same political attention
as climate change.**
The two crises are linked — habitat loss, invasive species, and pollution
drive extinctions that undermine ecosystem services that human economies
depend on. Canada's commitments under the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity
Framework require meaningful domestic implementation.

**4. Environmental data must be public and current.**
Environmental health cannot be evaluated without data. Water quality, habitat
extent, species population trends, contamination levels, and waste flows
should be published in accessible formats on predictable schedules. Citizens
cannot hold governments accountable for outcomes they cannot measure.

**5. Indigenous peoples are primary environmental stewards in many regions.**
Co-management arrangements, Indigenous guardianship programs, and recognition
of Indigenous territorial knowledge are not supplements to federal environmental
management — they are often more effective. Environmental policy should expand
and resource these relationships, not treat them as consultation checkboxes.

## Policy Mechanisms

**Federal contaminated sites remediation acceleration**
Publish a complete inventory of federally owned or federally liable contaminated
sites with current remediation status, estimated cost, and responsible party
identification. Set a binding schedule for remediation starts at all highest-risk
sites within five years. Where responsible parties are identifiable and solvent,
pursue full cost recovery through civil and regulatory enforcement. Where
parties are insolvent or unidentifiable, federal remediation proceeds without
waiting for resolution.

**Strengthened environmental liability rules**
Amend federal environmental legislation to close liability gaps that allow
responsible parties to transfer contaminated sites without adequate financial
assurance, or to use insolvency proceedings to avoid cleanup obligations.
Require environmental liability bonds or insurance as a condition of operating
permits for high-risk extractive and industrial activities.

**National biodiversity monitoring system**
Fund and operate a national biodiversity monitoring system covering species
population trends, habitat extent, invasive species spread, and ecosystem
integrity indicators. Data is published annually in machine-readable format
and independently reviewed. Progress against Kunming-Montreal Framework
targets is reported to Parliament and published publicly.

**Species at risk program reform**
Complete recovery strategies for listed species within legislated timelines —
currently a consistent area of non-compliance. Publish annual recovery plan
completion rates and population trend data for listed species. Critical
habitat protection orders are implemented within the legislative timeline
without requiring judicial enforcement to trigger them.

**Indigenous guardianship program expansion**
Expand the Indigenous Guardianship Program to fund Indigenous communities
to monitor, protect, and restore ecosystems within their territories.
Guardianship programs are governed by the community, not by federal program
rules, with federal funding conditional on basic accountability metrics
agreed in partnership. Co-management agreements receive dedicated legal
support from the federal government to address jurisdictional complexity.

**Circular economy and waste reduction incentives**
Expand federal procurement requirements for recycled content and end-of-life
product take-back in product categories where Canada has federal regulatory
jurisdiction. Publish annual federal waste data covering procurement, disposal,
and diversion rates. Federal funding for waste infrastructure (sorting, recovery,
composting) is available to municipalities meeting published diversion targets.

**Annual environmental accounts**
Publish annual federal environmental accounts covering: freshwater quality
and withdrawal rates, habitat extent by type, contamination load at monitored
sites, waste generation and diversion, and species population trends. Accounts
use a consistent methodology and are published in machine-readable formats
alongside narrative reporting. Methodology changes are disclosed.

---

## Measurable Outcomes

- Federal contaminated sites inventory is published and complete within
  18 months; remediation schedules published for all highest-risk sites
  within three years.
- Recovery strategies complete for 100% of listed species within legislated
  timelines within one governance cycle.
- National biodiversity monitoring data published annually, beginning within
  two years of program establishment.
- Indigenous Guardianship Program reaches at least 50 communities within
  three years, with co-management agreements reviewed and updated on schedule.
- Federal waste diversion rate improves cycle over cycle, with annual
  data published in machine-readable format.
- Critical habitat protection orders are implemented within legislated
  timelines for all newly listed species without judicial enforcement triggers.
