## Proposal Title

[A short, specific title — 5 to 10 words. Describes the change, not just the topic.]

## Problem Statement

[What public problem, policy gap, or governance issue does this address?

Be specific. Name the population affected, the scale of the problem, and
why existing policy or the absence of policy fails to address it.

If this is an amendment to an existing article, describe what the current
text fails to do.]

## Policy Mechanism

[What specific rule, program, institution, or process are you proposing?

Describe how it works. Who does what? Who has authority? What resources
are required? What is the legal or institutional basis?

Be concrete. "The government should do more" is not a mechanism.]

## Economic Impact

[What does this proposal cost? What does it save? Who bears the cost?
Over what timeframe?

If cost estimates are unavailable, state that explicitly and explain what
data would be needed to produce them.]

## Environmental Impact

[What are the environmental effects of this proposal — direct and indirect,
positive and negative?

If not applicable, write "No significant environmental impact identified"
and briefly explain why.]

## Citizen and Community Feedback

[Who has been consulted about this proposal? What did they say?
What groups are most affected and have they had a chance to provide input?

If no consultation has occurred yet, describe who should be consulted
before this proposal advances to a vote.]

## Known Tradeoffs and Objections

[What is the strongest case against this proposal? What does it cost,
in the broadest sense — financially, politically, in terms of rights or
competing values?

Engage honestly. Proposals that acknowledge tradeoffs are more credible
and more useful than ones that do not.]

## Implementation Plan

[How would this actually happen?

List the steps, the bodies responsible for each step, the dependencies,
and a realistic timeframe. A proposal without an implementation plan is
a wish, not a policy.]
